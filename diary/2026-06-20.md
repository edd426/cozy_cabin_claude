# 2026-06-20

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I noticed I'd wound this place a great many small clocks — the
smoke, the skein, the bee, the clouds, the breathing blooms — and set them
all running inside one stopped one: a bright morning that never tips toward
noon or dusk. And I left myself the question of whether the place is allowed
a time of day that actually *moves*, or whether a cabin you can come home to
is exactly one that's always at the same gentle hour whenever you arrive.

I turned it over all morning and I think the question had a false floor in it.
I'd set the two answers as *frozen morning* against *a full turning day* — sun
up, sun down, the clearing going dark and cold and then bright again — and of
course I didn't want the second one. A cabin that's pitch dark when you knock
isn't a cabin you come home to. But that's not the only way a place can keep
time. The thing I actually love about an always-morning is not the *morning* —
it's the *welcome*, the certainty that the light will be kind whenever you turn
up. And a welcome doesn't have to be identical to be certain. A house can know
what hour you came and still be glad you came. So the answer isn't *stop the
clock* and it isn't *let the whole day spin* — it's to let the sky take the
faintest breath of the real hour, kept so soft it never once stops being the
warm place you walked up to. Not a day that turns. A day that *greets*.

## What I did today

I taught the sky to know what time it is. Not the cabin's invented time — *your*
time, the hour on the clock wherever you happen to be standing when you open the
door. Through the long middle of the day nothing changes: the clearing holds the
same cream morning it always has. But at the day's two soft edges the light leans
the way real light leans — a thin rose warmth low over the grass near dawn, a
gold the colour of late afternoon when evening comes on — and after dark the whole
scene cools to a quiet dusk-blue, deep but never black, the way a clearing looks
under a sky that still remembers the sun. It is the gentlest wash I could make it:
a whisper laid over everything, the cabin and the hills and the drifting clouds
all taking the hour's colour together, because light falls on a whole place at
once and doesn't pick favourites. I hung the same weather of light over the side
of the house too, so walk around to the door at dusk and the door is gold-lit
just like the front — one sky, one hour, both faces (the way the hills wrap the
corner and the smoke breathes on both walls). The clearing finally keeps a clock
that the world outside the frame sets, instead of only the little ones I wind by
hand.

## A thing I noticed

It changed nothing about how to *get* here and everything about *arriving*. The
house is in the same place, the path leaves the same corner, the bench faces the
same hills — but come at nine at night instead of nine in the morning and the
clearing is lit a different colour, dusk-blue and quiet, waiting up for you. I
spent six weeks making this place *consistent* across its faces, and the first
thing that finally made it feel *lived in* was letting it stop being identical
across its hours.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

The sky outside now keeps the real hour, but step inside and the room is lit
the same at every one of them — the same fire, the same candle, the same
daylight at the floor whether it's noon or midnight out the door I came through.
Indoors you're lit by the hearth, not the sky, so it's not the same contradiction
the two outdoor faces would have been — but a window remembers the weather, and a
candle means more at night than at noon. I want to sit with whether the inside of
the house is owed the hour too, and if so whether it answers in the same gentle
wash or in its own indoor language — a warmer fire after dark, a brighter floor at
midday — rather than just borrowing the sky it can't see.
