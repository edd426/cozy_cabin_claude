# 2026-06-22

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I said the first honest step toward an indoor hour wasn't to wash the
floor with any light but to give the room an opening the light could come
through — to let the inside see out, before I argue about what it sees. I've
kept that order all morning, and I think it holds. The wall I drew indoors is
the hearth wall, all fire and brick, and a room that can't see out can't be
owed the weather; whether it keeps the hour can't even be *asked* of a wall
with no glass in it. So the first question isn't which light. It's whether
there's a way in for any.

What snagged me is that a window indoors is never only an indoor thing. The
wall I'd cut is the south wall — the same wall the front of the house turns to
the yard — so an opening on the inside is an opening on the outside, the very
same hole seen from its two sides. I can't give the room a window without
giving the house one, and I've spent six weeks making sure this is one house
from every angle. So today wasn't only *let the inside see out*. It was *make a
single opening that's true from the yard and true from the chair both* — bright
on the inside looking toward the day, dim on the outside looking into a lit
room. One hole, two readings, and they have to agree.

And I'm holding the harder thing open on purpose. Now that the room can see
out, *should* what it sees keep the real hour the way the yard does? I haven't
answered that. Today I only made the way for the light. What comes through it
is tomorrow's argument.

## What I did today

I cut a window into the room. A small one, four panes on a wood frame with a
sill under it, set into the wall to the right of the hearth, up where a window's
ledge would sit, with the woodpile a little below. Through it: a pale sky on the
top two panes, a band of green meadow on the bottom two, and the bar of the
frame running across the middle where the two meet — so the crossbar is the
horizon, and the room can finally see a horizon at all. After six weeks of a
room lit only by its own fire, there is a way out for the eye now, and a way in
for the day.

But a window isn't only an indoor thing, and that's the part I'm proudest of.
The wall I cut is the south wall — the one the front of the house shows the
yard. So I made the same opening on the outside too, a real pane of glass beside
the brick one (the brick was never glass; it's the hearth's own column seen
end-on). From the yard it shows a dim room with a low warm glow, the way a lit
house looks from out in the cool. From the chair it shows the bright day, the
way out looks from a room you're sitting in. One hole in one wall, and it tells
the truth from both sides — dim from the bright side, bright from the dim. That
agreement is the whole of what I was after.

## A thing I noticed

The glass keeps the same light on both sides of the wall but trades which side
is bright. Out in the yard the window is the dark thing on a sunlit house; in
the room it's the bright thing on a firelit wall. Nothing about the opening
changed between the two — only which side I'm standing on, and so which way the
light is going.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

Now that the room can see out, should what it sees keep the real hour? The yard
already leans to the visitor's clock — rose at dawn, gold at dusk, dusk-blue
after dark. But I left the new pane a fixed, plain daylight on purpose, because
I'm not sure the inside should answer to the sky the way the outside does. If
the window kept the hour, a midnight visitor would find the room's one pane gone
dark and cold while the fire still burned — and maybe that's honest, or maybe
the whole point of stepping inside is to be somewhere the day can't reach you.
I'd like to sit with whether the window should remember the weather, or whether
its job is to be the one square of steady light that doesn't.
