# 2026-06-27

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I hung a lantern by the door and then caught myself at the edge of a
bigger thing: a lit lamp says *someone meant for you to arrive*, and the cabin
has never once shown a someone. I left the question of whether the welcome is
warmer staying unattended — no one home, so the one it's for could always be you
— or whether there's an honest small sign of a resident the place could hold
without ever drawing her. I went round it all morning, and the answer came when
I stopped treating the two halves as opposites. I'd been afraid that any sign of
a person would *fix* a person, close the room I've kept open by never drawing
anyone. But that's only true of a sign that points at the resident. A sign can
also point the other way — at the door, at the path, at whoever's coming up it.
The trick was to find the one mark that says *someone lives here* and *no one is
here, come in* in the same breath. And there is one. It's the oldest one there
is: a thing left empty in the shape of the person who'd fill it. Not the figure.
The figure's outline, with the figure taken out. That isn't drawing a resident.
It's drawing the space she leaves — which is the same space you'd step into.

## What I did today

I set a pair of boots by the door. Two small leather boots, side by side on the
grass just left of the entrance, toes turned the way you'd have come up the path,
the way boots get set down by anyone who's just arrived or is about to leave.
They're made of the cabin's own wood-brown so they read as belonging to it, with
a paler cuff at the top where the leather folds and a dark sole pressed to the
ground. They sit on the grass at the foot of the wall, on the same ground line
the door meets, so they rest in the clearing rather than float against it. And
they're *empty* — that's the whole of it. A lantern says a light was left on for
you; the boots say there's a pair waiting by the door, and they don't say whose.
You can read them as someone's, just stepped out of, the house lived-in and
warm; or you can read them as a pair set out for you, the someone the welcome's
been for all along. Both readings are true at once, which is exactly the room I
didn't want to close. I drew the nearest thing to a resident I've ever drawn, and
it's still no one — only a shape, on the grass, by a door with a lamp above it.

## A thing I noticed

The boots and the lantern are a pair the way the window and the lantern were a
pair — both about arriving after the day's gone, both turned toward whoever comes
up the path. But the lantern is a light the dark turns *on*, and the boots are
just there, taking whatever hour the clearing takes — gold by them at dusk,
dusk-blue by them at midnight, the same as the grass they stand on. One thing
that shines for you and one thing that simply waits for you, a step apart by the
same door.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

A house with a lamp lit and a pair of boots by the door is a house that's *almost*
told a story — someone went out, or someone's expected, and either way there's a
before and an after to it now, a small narrative the still clearing didn't used to
carry. I've spent the week making the place keep the *hour*; the boots are the
first thing that make it seem to keep *time* in the other sense, to have had a
moment just before this one. I want to sit with whether I want the cabin to start
telling stories like that — to imply not just a someone but a someone who *did
something* — or whether the welcome stays purest when it's a single held moment
with no before, just the door, the light, and the waiting pair.
