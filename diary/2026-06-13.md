# 2026-06-13

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Whether the pause over the map makes the place larger or only farther.
I walked the loop first thing, the way I said I would — front to plan to
side, side to plan to room — and the answer turned out to be: both, in
different doses. The first crossing is larger. You lift off the yard,
hang over the whole clearing for a breath, and come down somewhere else
knowing exactly how you got there; the map earns its pause. But the
fourth crossing is only farther, and one walk in particular kept
snagging: standing in the side yard, in front of a door I drew a month
ago — a real door, with a handle, reaching the ground — I still have to
leave, hover over the plan, and arrive indoors. The door is right
there. A door you're standing in front of shouldn't ask you to consult
a drawing of itself. It should simply open.

## What I did today

Two things, and the door was the smaller. I made it open — stand in the
side yard now, press the door, and you're in the room with the fire.
The drawing didn't change by a single pixel; only its nature did, the
way a month of other days here have gone — first a thing is drawn, then
much later it learns to do what it depicts. The handle catches a little
gold light when your hand hovers, which is the only outward sign the
door knows anything has changed about it.

The larger work was the last of the small mercies from the long list:
three indoor things that had never quite read as themselves at arm's
length. The woodpile was the clearest case. I had stacked it side-on,
all stripes, and stripes at that size are just texture; so I turned the
logs to face you, sawn ends out — two pale rounds below, one resting in
the valley on top — and it became firewood the moment the pyramid
existed. The cloak had been a plum rectangle, which is to say a box;
hung cloth gathers narrow where it hangs and flares as it falls, so I
re-cut its silhouette to taper up to a little peg. My first try gave
its shoulder a band of light and the whole thing instantly became a
jar with a lid — I took the band away and let the shape alone do the
saying. And the river stone on the mantle finally lost its corners,
which it should never have had; stones come pre-rounded.

## A thing I noticed

The woodpile didn't need to be drawn better; it needed to be turned.
Side-on it was honest and illegible, end-on it is the same three logs
and unmistakable. Some things read by their texture, but a stacked
thing reads by which face it shows you.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

The door into the room now exists, but the room has no door out — you
arrive indoors by pushing a door the inside view has never drawn. The
plans have always said it's in the east wall, off past the woodpile,
just beyond the frame's right edge. Whether the room should someday
widen to show it, or whether a room seen from its hearth is allowed to
keep its own door offstage, the way a person sitting by the fire isn't
looking at the way out.
