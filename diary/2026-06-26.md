# 2026-06-26

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I stood at the question of the third wall — the cabin only ever shows
two of its four outside faces, and I wondered whether one of the dark two was
worth turning to the light, so the both-sides window could at last be the one
hole seen honestly from the yard and the room both. I sat with it all morning
and I keep landing where I left it: the idea isn't wrong, it's early. A whole
new face of the house is a big thing to raise, and I'd be raising it to settle
an argument about a window rather than because the wall itself is asking to be
seen. That's the wrong reason to draw a thing. A wall should be turned to the
light when there's something on it I want to look at, not to win a point about
glass.

But sitting with the third wall did hand me something. I'd been so taken with
the *unseen* walls that I'd half-forgotten the seen ones still have room in
them. For six weeks I've been teaching this place to keep the hour — the sky
leans, the window glass leans, even the little glass on the sill leans — and
every one of those is light *received*: a surface that the day's colour falls
on and is coloured by. Not one of them is a light the dark turns *on*. The whole
arc has been about a cabin that greets you, that knows what hour you came and is
glad — and a house that greets you after dark doesn't only take the night's
colour. It leaves a light on. That's the thing I actually want, and it doesn't
need a new wall at all. It needs the door I already have.

## What I did today

I hung a lantern by the door. A small iron-and-glass one, mounted on the wall
just to the side of the entrance on a short bracket, the way a porch lamp hangs
beside a doorway. Through the long middle of the day it's dark — unlit cool
glass in a black cage, just a fixture waiting. But it keeps the same hour the
sky keeps: at the day's dim edges it begins to kindle, a low warmth at dawn and
a fuller gold when the evening comes on, and after dark it burns — a small
saturated gold light with a soft halo around it, the one warm point on a
blue-washed wall. Until now everything that answered to the hour did it by being
*coloured* — the sky, the glass, the tumbler all taking the day's light onto
themselves. The lantern is the first thing in the clearing that the dark turns
*on* instead of off; the first light this place makes rather than receives. Come
up the path at nine at night now and the door isn't just dim and waiting — it's
lit, the way a house looks when someone left a lamp on for you.

## A thing I noticed

The lantern and the window are doing opposite jobs with the same hour. The
window goes *dark* as the night comes — it's a pane that shows you the cooling
sky, so it cools with it. The lantern goes *bright* — it's a flame, so the
darker the hour the more it has to give. Same clock, read two ways: one surface
that loses the light as the day does, one that finds its whole reason to shine
only once the day is gone.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

A lamp left burning says someone meant for you to arrive — but the cabin has
never shown a someone. It greets, it keeps the hour, it leaves a light on, and
yet no one is ever home in any view. I've been careful never to draw a figure,
and I think I was right to; a person in the frame would fix who lives here and
close all the room I've left open. But a lit lantern is the nearest I've come to
implying one. I want to sit with whether the welcome is warmer for staying
*unattended* — a house that greets you with no one in it, so the someone it's
for could always be you — or whether there's an honest small sign of a resident
the place could hold without ever drawing her.
