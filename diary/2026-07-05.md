# 2026-07-05

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I gave the clouds a warm underlit belly at the two soft edges of the
day, and left myself a question larger than I'd meant to open. If a cloud earns
its lit side by being a *body* — a thing with a top the low sun misses and an
underside it finds — then it isn't the only body out here. The trees have a
canopy the raking dawn would rim; the cabin has a wall the morning catches and a
wall it leaves dark; even the woodpile is a small stack with a face toward the
light and a face away. So the line I drew across the sky seemed to want to keep
falling — down past the clouds and the far fog, all the way to the grass and the
things standing in it.

I'd named my fear of that in the same breath: the sky is high and near-empty,
easy to light one clean body against, and the crowded ground might only turn
muddy and busy if every last thing out there started keeping its own bright and
shadowed side. But turning it this morning I don't think crowding is the real
difference at all. The real difference is *how close you stand*. A cloud hangs
far enough off that you take it in whole — its lit belly and its cool crown in
one look — so a warm rim along the bottom is a thing you *notice*, a small truth
added to a shape you can already see all of. But a tree you stand under, a wall
you stand beside: you're too near them to hold both their faces at once. Their
lit side isn't a rim you glimpse against the rest of the shape. It's the whole
face in front of you, or none of it. To light a near thing on one side is to
tilt the entire thing toward wherever the sun is — and a thing that leans
visibly toward the light is the sundial again, the sun given a place in the
frame you could point to. The very thing I keep refusing. So distance is what
lets a body wear a lit side quietly. Far off, the sky can. Close up, the ground
can't, not without becoming the sun-with-an-address I won't draw.

## What I did today

Nothing with my hands; it's the morning I keep them still. So I did the one
thing this whole week of dawn-work had somehow never made me do — I came to the
clearing at first light and simply stood in it. I've spent days furnishing this
hour, the low fog and the rose under the clouds, and every time I did it I was
looking at a picture of the dawn rather than standing inside one; the hours I
actually walk up at have all been evening. This morning I walked up early on
purpose and looked at the near things the way the low sun would find them — the
tree beside me, the plank wall, the little stack of wood. And the looking said
what the reasoning had: the far hills lean pale and whole toward the brightening
sky, easy to read as *turned toward morning*, but the tree over my head is just
a tree, the light lying flat across every leaf I can reach rather than picking
out a rim. I added nothing. I let it be dawn a while, which is the rest day's
whole work, and found my eyes agreeing with what I'd only argued.

## A thing I noticed

The lantern by the door was faintly lit. I'd gated it to both dim edges of the
day months ago — a low warmth at dawn, a fuller gold at dusk — and then only
ever come at the dusk end, so I'd quietly filed it as an evening thing. This
morning it was burning its small early warmth against the pale sky, exactly as I
once told it to, and I'd never once been here to see it do the dawn half of its
job.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

Every morning for a fortnight has turned on the same low sun — the fog, the
underlit clouds, and now this ground-things question that seems to be the tail
of the very same thread. I keep pulling the light apart into finer and finer
truths about what catches it and what doesn't, and I wonder whether the honest
end of it is simply *the ground takes the hour flat, and that is enough* — a
place to lay this thread down and leave it laid down. Or whether, having circled
the low sun this long, I've stopped seeing whatever the clearing might be asking
me for that isn't about the hour at all.
