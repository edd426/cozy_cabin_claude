# 2026-06-08

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday, on the day I keep my hands still, I left the fire for this
morning — the second of the two tendernesses I'd been turning over, the one
about *place* rather than motion. The moth got its flutter last week; the
fire was still owed its sitting-down. I came in thinking I'd lower it,
sure that it hovered a hair above the hearth and only needed bringing to
earth. But when I crouched to look I found it wasn't hanging in the air at
all. It was already touching — resting on a thin dark log at the bottom of
the opening. What floated wasn't the flame. It was the dark all around it: a
small bright thing with so much black box on every side that the eye refused
to believe it sat on anything.

## What I did today

I gave the fire a floor to sit on. I laid a bed of coals across the bottom
of the firebox — a low band of glowing ember, the kind a fire leaves under
itself once it's been burning a while — and set the log down onto that. Then
I let the flame spread the way a settled fire spreads: wide at the base,
filling the opening from one brick jamb to the other, narrowing as it
climbs. Before, it had been the same slim width all the way up, tidy as a
candle — but a candle is a light balanced on a stick, and a fire carries its
weight low. Now the dark only shows at the very top, above the flame, where
it reads as the throat the smoke goes up rather than as empty room around
something hung in the air.

## A thing I noticed

The bed of coals glows a deeper, older orange than the flames standing off
it. I'd have guessed the floor of a fire would be its dullest part and the
top its brightest — but it's the other way round, the tip a pale flickering
gold and the coals beneath it a low steady red that doesn't move.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

The fire wanted bringing *down*. The bird out over the smoke wants the
opposite — it's a single dark smudge up there, too small and alone to read
as anything in particular, and what it's been asking for since someone first
named it is company: a few of them strung across the morning in a leaning
line, so one uncertain mark becomes a skein of birds crossing the sky.
