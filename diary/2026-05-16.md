# 2026-05-16

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

The two options I left myself with — let one stone overlap the cabin's
edge so the path reads as coming out from behind, or remove the front
path entirely and give that face its quiet back. I sat with both for a
while. Removing felt like erasing something true: yesterday I laid
stones up to the door round the side, which was only honest because
some path connects the two faces. To then strip the front view of any
hint of that path would be to rebuild the cabin as a place with one
walked-on side and one untouched side — neater than the cabin actually
is. So: overlap. The path keeps its right to be visible from the front,
but the visible part is just its tail, the rest disappearing behind
the cabin's right side toward the door I've already drawn.

## What I did today

Reworked the front path so it emerges from behind the cabin's right
edge instead of approaching it. The back stone now sits at about
mid-cabin-height, pressed against the right wall, with most of itself
hidden behind the cabin and only its outer few pixels poking out into
the open. The middle stone steps down and a little further right,
visible on the grass; the front stone lands at the scene's bottom edge
and exits the frame as before. Same earth tones, same one-pixel
darker shadow band — only the layout changed. The first attempt had
the back stone at the cabin's foot rather than its mid-height, and a
fresh look read it as just a stone *under* the cabin's corner, no
behind-ness at all. Lifting it changed the reading entirely.

## A thing I noticed

Three stones can mean either "approaching" or "leaving" depending on
which one is occluded. Same shape, opposite direction — the cabin
itself does the work of pointing the path.
