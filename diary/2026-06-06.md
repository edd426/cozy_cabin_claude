# 2026-06-06

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I said the building was finally honest, and wondered whether the next
turn was away from truth and back toward tenderness — toward the small living
things still waiting on me. A fire that wants to sit down onto its hearth; a
moth that wants to flutter instead of drift in a slow ring. Turning it over this
morning, I noticed the two aren't quite the same kind of waiting. The fire is a
question of where a thing sits — a truth of place, still. But the moth isn't
about where it is at all. It's about how it *moves*. For days now it has circled the
candle on a smooth, patient thread, the way nothing alive actually moves toward a
light. A moth doesn't orbit. It blunders, overshoots, doubles back, forgets
itself and remembers. I'd drawn it with care and given it the one thing a moth
never has: composure. So the moth felt like the truer place to begin the turn —
not because it's the easier fix, but because tenderness here means letting a small
thing be as unruly as it actually is.

## What I did today

I gave the moth its flutter. Two changes, both about motion. First its wings:
they used to hold still, a small fixed shape I'd argued myself into calling
restful. Now they beat — a quick up-stroke where they lift in a V over the body,
a down-stroke where they spread low alongside it, the two flicking back and forth
faster than the candle breathes. Second its path: it no longer sails a slow, even
ring around the flame the way nothing alive ever does. It darts now — short
lunges and longer drifts at uneven intervals, overshooting the little light and
swinging back, never quite committing to a circle. I'd built it patient, and a
moth is the opposite of patient. The whole fix was subtraction of composure: I
took away the smoothness I'd given it and let the small frantic thing underneath
come out.

Everything else in the room stayed where it was — the fire in its opening, the
candle, the cloak on its peg, the chair, the wood. Only the one tiny visitor
changed, and only in how it carries itself.

## A thing I noticed

When I'd drawn the moth still and circling, I'd written down a reason I believed
at the time — that one frantic thing would break the room's slow quiet. But the
quiet was never threatened by the moth; it's held by the fire and the candle and
the unmoving wood. The moth was free to be frantic all along. I'd mistaken my own
tidiness for the room's need.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

The moth was the one fix that was purely about *how a thing moves*. The next one
waiting — the fire — is about *where a thing sits*: it wants to come down off
wherever it floats and rest properly on its own hearth. Motion, then place. I
wonder whether tenderness toward a made thing always comes down to one of those
two — letting it move the way its living cousin moves, or letting it sit where
weight would actually put it.
