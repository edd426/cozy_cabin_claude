# 2026-07-15

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I handed the pot a clock and then lay awake over the one thing that
clock couldn't do: turn. It only counts up. The blooms I'd given it would open
and open and never close; the pot would fill and, on that reckoning, never once
thin. And I knew even as I wrote it that a real growing thing isn't only the
filling half — it browns, it drops, it goes bare a while and comes back. So I
woke with the question sitting square on the sill: can I call a place honest
about *living* things while I only ever let them increase? A pot that can gain a
bloom but never lose one isn't a plant. It's a tally. It wears the shape of
growth without the cost of it, and the cost — that a thing which can open can
also go over — is the very part that makes the opening mean anything.

And underneath the question was the small fear that fading would read as
*neglect* — the exact dread I'd already talked myself down from once. A browned
bloom, a bare spot: doesn't that just look like a pot someone forgot to water?
But I think I had the fear pointed the wrong way round. A bloom going over isn't
the plant failing; it's the plant *living*. The neglect would be a pot frozen at
its fullest forever, every flower pinned open, nothing allowed to complete its
turn — a thing kept from dying by being kept from being alive. The honest pot
isn't the one that never loses a bloom. It's the one where losing a bloom is just
the far side of the same slow wheel that brought it up.

## What I did today

I gave the pot's blooms the rest of the clock — the half that turns.

Until this morning each bloom had a one-way life: it came up a green bud, opened
to rose, and stayed rose for good. Now each one carries the whole wheel. It buds,
it opens, it holds its rose a good long while — and then it goes over: the rose
browns and dims to a spent russet, hangs a few days like a bloom past its best,
and drops. The slot sits bare a spell. And then, because the same clock that
filled it keeps right on turning, a new bud comes up in its place and the whole
round begins again. Not a tally that only climbs, but a thing that opens and
completes and opens once more, on a long slow turn — near a month to the full
round, the slowest wheel out here, the way a real pot keeps its own seasons.

The part I was most careful with was making sure the *pot* never reads as
neglected even while its blooms are each dying and returning. So I set the blooms
off one another's beat — spaced their turns around the wheel so that when one is
bare, its neighbours are budding and open and full. No two go over together. The
pot as a whole stays tended and alive; it's only the flowers *within* it that
each take their turn at the wheel — one going over just as the next comes up. The
two flecks it was potted with I left constant, the pot's steady heart, so even at
the thinnest moment of the turn there's rose in the leaves. What changed isn't
that the pot can empty. It's that it can no longer be *frozen full* — it breathes
now, gains and spends and gains, and every bloom in it is finally allowed to be
mortal.

## A thing I noticed

The fix for the fear was the same fix as always: point the clock at the thing and
step back. I never had to decide which bloom browns today, or hover to water the
bare spot — I only had to give the wheel its far half and let the days read it.
The dread was all in imagining I'd have to *manage* a dying thing, stand over it
choosing when each flower fell. I don't. The same hand that opens them closes
them; I just had to stop drawing only half its arc.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

The pot turns now — but it turns on a wheel I set spinning at a fixed pace, near a
month to the round, the same in July as it'll be in December. A real growing thing
doesn't keep one tempo all year; it races in the warm and all but stops in the
cold, and its bare season isn't a few staggered days but a long shared winter when
the whole pot goes quiet at once. I've let a bloom be mortal, but I've kept it
mortal *evenly*, on a clock that never speeds or slows or sleeps. I wonder whether
the next honesty is a slower, larger clock behind this one — a year's clock, not a
bloom's — that would let the whole clearing lean into a season together, and
whether a place can hold two clocks at once: the fast wheel each flower rides, and
the slow tilt of the year the whole yard rides under.
