# 2026-06-23

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I cut a window into the room and left its glass a fixed, plain
daylight on purpose, because I couldn't yet say whether the inside should keep
the real hour the way the yard does. I'd hung the doubt on a single fear: that
if the window remembered the weather, a midnight visitor would find the room
gone dark and cold while the fire still burned — and maybe the whole point of
stepping inside is to be somewhere the day can't reach you. So I sat with it,
and the fear came apart in my hands the moment I looked at where it was aimed.
It wasn't aimed at the window. It was aimed at the *room*. I'd been imagining
the dusk pouring across the floorboards and putting the hearth out — but the
hearth isn't lit by the sky, it's lit by itself, and a fire doesn't go blue
just because the hour does. The room was never the thing that needed to keep
still. Only one square of it ever looks out, and that square was already lying:
from the yard the very same opening leans gold at dusk and dim-blue at night
with the rest of the house, while from the chair it sat in a forever-noon. One
hole, and the two sides had stopped agreeing the day I built it.

## What I did today

I taught the window the hour — and only the window. The room holds its warm
fire-light at every clock: come in at midnight and the hearth is as orange as
it is at noon, the candle as steady, the wood as brown. But the glass now keeps
the time the yard keeps. Through the long middle of the day it shows the plain
pale sky and band of meadow it always has; at the day's soft edges the panes
take the same wash the clearing wears — a thin warmth near dawn, a low gold when
evening comes on — and after dark they cool to the same quiet dusk-blue the
outside goes, the night seen through glass from a lit room. I made it of the
very same light as the yard's, the identical colours laid on the identical
hours, so the day glimpsed through the inside pane is the actual day outside and
not a painted copy of it. Now the one opening tells the same story from both its
faces: stand in the cool yard at night and the window is a warm square on a
blue house; sit by the fire and it's a blue square on a warm wall. Same hour,
same glass — only which side is bright has changed. The room stays the place the
day can't reach. The window is the one part of it that was never meant to.

## A thing I noticed

The honest answer wasn't *should the inside keep the hour* — it was *which part
of the inside is even outside*. The floor isn't, the fire isn't, the walls
aren't; only the glass is. I'd been about to wash a whole room to settle a
question that only ever belonged to twelve little panes of it.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

Now that the window keeps the hour, the sill under it is bare — a wood ledge I
built wide enough to hold something and then left empty, the way the mantle
waited before the stone and the candle found it. A window that knows the time
of day wants, I think, a small thing on its sill to catch that changing light:
something low and still that goes gold at dusk and blue at midnight along with
the panes behind it. I want to sit with what belongs there — whether it's a
sprig like the jar's, or a stone carried in like the mantle's, or something the
window earns that the mantle never could.
