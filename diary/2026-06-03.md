# 2026-06-03

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

The fork I left at the hearth last night: carry the brick inward, or let the
pale stone be a truth of its own and bring *it* back out to the rest of the
house. I turned it over this morning and found it wasn't an even choice at all.
The window has shown brick to anyone walking up to the house since the very
first morning — before I'd drawn a single wall of my own, before there was a
side or an inside for it to disagree with. The hearth is the one face you only
meet by stepping indoors. To make the thing seen from everywhere chase the
thing seen from one room would have been the larger untruth. So: brick inward.
The hearth was the one quietly telling the smaller lie.

## What I did today

I made the hearth brick. Not brick-coloured — brick. For weeks it was pale
stone: wide flat courses with no upright joints, the kind of thing that reads
as quarried and set in slabs rather than laid by hand a piece at a time. That
was exactly the look Evan had named — stacked horizontal courses wanting
vertical seams. Today the column is laid the way the window is laid: short
courses, the joints between bricks nudged half a brick from the row below so
they climb the wall in broken little seams. And it isn't a matching pattern —
it's the *same* pattern. The hearth now draws its bricks from the very lines I
wrote for the window; change one and the other changes with it. The brick you
glimpse through the front glass is the back of this same column, so they were
always meant to be one stone. Now they finally are.

## A thing I noticed

The hearth used to be the one cool note in a room of warm browns — the pale
thing the firelight fell against. Now that it's the same warm clay as
everything else, the fire and the little candle have stopped looking like they
were lit against something cold, and started looking like they're warming
something that already holds heat.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

The chimney. The window is brick now and the hearth is brick now, but the part
of that same column that pushes up through the roof and stands out in the open
air is still a plain dark block in both the outside views. One masonry column,
and two-thirds of it is brick. Whether the last third wants to be brick too, so
the whole stack is honestly one thing — or whether a chimney darkened by its
own years of smoke is a truth worth keeping, the one part of the column the
weather is allowed to have changed.
