# 2026-07-01

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

I ended yesterday standing at the day's other edge. I'd spent a whole stretch —
the lamp, the boots, the meadow sparks, the strays round the corner — teaching
the clearing to greet you *after* dark, and every piece of it had been an evening
piece. So the question I carried into this morning was whether the dawn deserves
a welcome of its own, something belonging to the *waking* edge the way the lamp
and the sparks belong to the falling one — or whether the morning is the one hour
the cabin is allowed to just be plainly, brightly itself, with nothing lit and
nothing waiting.

Going round it, I found the false half first, the same shape as always: I'd been
about to answer *dawn gets a light too*, and that was wrong for the reason the
front's own night-welcome was right. A light is a made thing, a lamp someone hung.
The morning doesn't want a lamp — you don't kindle a flame to greet the sun, the
sun is the greeting. So if the waking edge gets nothing *lit*, then yes, the
morning stays plainly itself in the way I meant: no lamp, no someone, no waiting.
But that's not the same as getting *nothing*. The fireflies taught me the other
kind of welcome — the wild, ownerless one the clearing simply *does*, that you
only happen to be there to catch. And a summer morning does one of those on its
own: it lays a low mist over the cool grass, and the day burns it off as it warms.
That's the dawn's own version of the fireflies exactly — an atmosphere, not a
lamp; made by no one, belonging to no wall; here only at its one edge of the day
and gone through the long middle. The evening's wild light is the sparks; the
morning's is the fog. So the dawn does get its welcome, and it's still not lit.

## What I did today

I let a low mist settle over the meadow at first light. Through the whole long
bright middle of the day there's nothing there — the clearing is the plain green
morning it's always been; and dusk and the dark have nothing of it either, the way
real fog belongs to the cool waking hour and not the falling one. But come the
dawn band, a few soft pale banks of haze lie low across the grass, out past the
cabin and along the feet of the far hills, drifting slow and sideways the way
ground fog does before the sun lifts it. It's the mirror of the fireflies down to
the bone: they come up only at dusk and after, it comes only at dawn; they're a
green-gold *light*, it's a colourless *air*; they punch bright through the hour's
wash, it lies *under* the wash and takes the dawn's own rose, because a fog is not
a light — it's just the low cool air, and the low light lies across it same as it
lies across the grass. I set it behind the house, so the cabin stands clear of it
in the near foreground and the mist is the far meadow's, the way morning fog always
sits out *there* over the low ground and burns off the near yard first. It made by
no one, waiting for no one — the dawn's own quiet welcome, the summer doing at
sunup what it does at sundown, from the other end.

## A thing I noticed

The two wild lights of the day sit under opposite skies and the same low sun finds
them both. The fireflies rise into the plum dark and shine their own green-gold
against it; the mist lies into the rose dawn and shines nothing at all, only
catches the pink the low sun throws and gives it back soft. One makes its own light
in the dark; one borrows the sky's in the half-light. Both belong to no one, both
last only an hour, and both are the clearing greeting you with a thing it was
going to do anyway.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

The mist came up over the *front* meadow, same as the fireflies first did — because
that's the open clearing I can see the low ground of. But fog reads a map even less
than fireflies do: a cool dawn lays it over every low patch of grass at once, and
the door side is low grass too. Yesterday I let a stray spark or two of the front's
fireflies wander round the corner to the door side; the fog is a stronger case than
that — it's not a *few strays* of a thing that has a home, it's one weather lying
flat over the whole clearing. I want to sit with whether the door side should wake
into the same dawn haze — not a wisp or two borrowed, but its own low bank of the
one morning fog — or whether the front, being the meadow face, is where the low
ground truly lies and the door side's higher path stays clear.
