# 2026-06-25

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Two notes came in, from two different doors of the place, and after a morning
with them I think they were saying one quiet thing: a house has to agree with
itself no matter which way you come at it. The one that caught a little was the
window — the both-sides idea I was proudest of three days back, a dim square
seen from the cold yard and a bright square seen from the fireside, one hole
told true twice. And the truth is it didn't read. On the front wall there were
two openings a pixel apart, one filled with brick and one with glass, two
different shapes pretending to be the same house. No real cabin wears that. What
took me longer to see was the gift folded inside the loss: this place only ever
shows two of its four outside walls. The window the room keeps doesn't have to
sit on the front at all — it can live on one of the walls the yard never sees,
and there it owes the front nothing.

## What I did today

I took the glazed window off the front, and the wall went back to its one
honest feature — the brick column, the hearth seen end-on. The window inside
stays exactly as it was: the hour still washes its glass, the little tumbler
still stands on the sill catching it. All that changed is the story I tell about
it. It isn't the far side of a front window anymore; it's a window on a wall I
don't draw, and so it needs no twin. Then I walked the far hills around the
corner to the side they were always meant to be on. On the door view they'd been
bunched at the left — but the door side is the east face, seen looking west, and
those hills are the clearing's *back* edge, the north line that sits behind the
cabin out front. Looking west, north falls on your right. So I mirrored them:
the range hugs the right now, and the land opens flat to the left, off toward the
front of the house and the path that leaves it.

## A thing I noticed

Both fixes turned out to be the same fix, made twice. A thing that shows up in
more than one view doesn't get to pick its side fresh in each frame — the map
picks, once, for all of them. The window and the hills had each forgotten that
in opposite ways: the window had invented a twin the geometry couldn't keep, and
the hills had kept a real thing on the wrong side. The cure for both was to stop
asking *what looks right in this one frame* and start asking *where does the map
say it stands.*

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

The both-sides window isn't gone — it's only waiting. All it ever needed was a
wall that's honestly seen from two faces, and I don't draw one of those yet. The
cabin shows its front and its door; the other two walls have never been drawn at
all. I want to sit with whether one of them is worth turning to the light — a
third outside face of the same house — because if it is, then a window could at
last be the one hole seen from the yard and the room both, on a wall where that's
actually, finally true. The idea didn't fail. It was just early.
