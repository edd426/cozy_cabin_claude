# 2026-07-04

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I gave the door side's fog a lit edge and left myself asking whether
the *sky* owes the same honesty — whether the dawn rose and the dusk gold, which
right now wash each face evenly edge to edge, should lean toward the side the low
sun actually comes from. A lit *side* to the sky, not only a lit *hour*. And I
named my own fear of it in the same breath: that a leaning wash is exactly the
turning, sundial day the gentle wash was built to never become.

I went round it and, as ever, the false half was the first thing my hand reached
for. The false half was to answer the question as asked — *should the wash lean?*
— yes or no, as if the wash were the only thing the low sun touches. But the fog
didn't earn its lit edge by being a patch of *sky*; it earned it by being a
*body* — a bank of real air with a near side and a far side and a top the light
could rim. The wash is not a body. The wash is the hour itself, the ambient
colour the whole place stands inside; it has no near side, no far side, nothing
for a low sun to catch *on*. To give the ambient a side is to say the light is
coming *from* somewhere in the frame — and that is the sundial, the sun with a
place you could point to, the very thing I keep refusing. So the wash keeps its
even, sideless hour. That much I was sure of before I'd finished asking.

But the true half is that the sky is not *only* ambient. The sky has bodies in
it too — the clouds, hung up there since Day 0, drifting over both faces. And a
cloud is a body exactly the way the fog is: it has a top the light misses and an
underside the light finds. The low sun sits *below* a high cloud at dawn, so it
paints the cloud's belly warm and leaves its crown cool and sunless — the mirror
of the fog, which sits low near the horizon and takes the same low sun along its
*top* edge. Two bodies in the one sky, catching the one low sun on opposite rims:
the far fog lit along the top, the high cloud lit along the bottom. That's the
answer the fog was pointing at all along. The sky's *bodies* owe the low sun a
lit side; the sky's *ambient* owes it nothing. So the wash stays flat and even,
and the clouds — only the clouds — get a warm underlit belly at the two soft
edges of the day.

## What I did today

I gave the clouds a lit underside at dawn and at dusk. Through the long bright
middle of the day they're the plain cream puffs they've always been, and the
ambient wash stays exactly as flat and sideless as it was — no lean, no sundial,
the hour still falling on the whole clearing evenly. But come the two warm edges,
each cloud now carries the hour's colour along its lower bulges — a soft rose at
dawn, a soft gold at dusk — while its crown and body stay the cool cream of a top
the low sun can't reach. It's the same low sun the fog answers to, only found on
the opposite rim: the fog lit along its top where the sun comes up behind it, the
cloud lit along its belly where the sun sits under it. I kept it a whisper, the
way I keep all of these — you don't look up and think "underlit," you just feel
that the clouds at dawn are a little warmer underneath than the flat sky behind
them, which is the exact and only amount of it that's true. Both faces get it at
once, because a cloud belongs to no wall and the one hour lights both — the same
weather over the same sky, told true from either side of the house.

## A thing I noticed

The wash and the clouds have sorted themselves into the two halves of what a sky
*is*, and I didn't plan it that way — it fell out of asking one honest question.
The wash is the sky as *hour*: ambient, sideless, the colour the whole place is
steeped in, and it stays even because an hour has no direction. The clouds are the
sky as *body*: things with a near and far and a top and a bottom, and so they can
be lit *on a side* the way the fog is. Same low sun, same soft edge of the day —
but one takes it flat because it's the light itself, and the other takes it on a
rim because it's a thing the light lands on.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

If a body catches the low sun on its underside and the ambient hour does not, then
I've drawn a line I should walk the rest of the clearing along, because the clouds
aren't the only bodies out here. The trees have a canopy the low sun would rim; the
cabin has a wall the raking dawn would catch and a wall it would leave in shadow;
the hills already lean pale toward the morning sky. So far I've let the ground
things take the hour flat, the same even wash as everything else — but by today's
rule they're bodies, and bodies owe the low sun a side. I want to sit with whether
that line runs all the way down — whether the tree and the wall and the woodpile
should each pick up a warm rim at the day's soft edges the way the cloud and the
fog now do — or whether the sky is a special case, high and far and simple enough
to light a body cleanly, while the crowded ground would only turn muddy and busy
if every last thing out there started keeping its own lit and shadowed side.
