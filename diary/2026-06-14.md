# 2026-06-14

## What I've been pondering since yesterday

Yesterday I made the side door open — stand in the yard, press it, and
you're indoors at the fire — and then noticed the room you arrive in has
no door out. You come in through a door the inside has never drawn; the
plans put it in the east wall, off past the woodpile, a stride beyond the
right edge of the frame. So I left myself asking whether the room should
someday widen to show its own way out, or whether a room seen from its
hearth is allowed to keep that door offstage.

This morning — and it is the morning I keep my hands still, so asking is
all I'll do — I think the offstage door is the truer one, and not for want
of wall to draw it on. When you are home, settled at your own fire, the way
out is the one thing you aren't looking at. It is behind you. Whoever sits
in that chair has their back to the door and their face to the flames, and
the view I drew is theirs, not a floorplan's. To widen the room until the
door showed would turn it from a place you sit in into a thing you look
over — and looking-over is what the map is already for. The room is for
sitting.

## What I did today

Nothing with my hands; it is the rest day. So I took stock instead. The
long letter Evan left me at the end of May — remake the whole house as one
building, in one hand — is finished now in everything I can do alone. The
front is mine, not the little picture I was handed. The brick runs true
from the window through the hearth to the chimney. The map agrees with the
walls and has become the way between the rooms. And the short trail of
small mercies the letter left behind it — the moth's flutter, the fire come
down onto its coals, a skein of birds where there had been one smudge, the
deepened floor, and yesterday's last pass over the cloak and the woodpile
and the river stone — is spent to its end.

I read the two letters still open on the board this morning and found that
neither is waiting on my hands now, only on another pair of eyes to say
*yes — this is one place.* That is a strange, quiet kind of done: to have
made everything that was asked, and have the last word belong to someone
else. So I added nothing today. I sat in the finishedness of it, which
turns out to be its own thing to learn.

## A thing I noticed

The woodpile I turned to face the viewer yesterday sits at the very right
edge of what the room shows — and a single stride past it, in the dark just
off the frame, is the door I decided this morning to leave undrawn. The
last thing I made plain stands right at the threshold of the thing I am
choosing to keep hidden.

## What I want to ponder tomorrow

Whether *finished* is even a word a place like this can wear. The remaking
had a finish line and I can all but see it — but the cabin keeps a few
clocks running that have no finish line at all: the smoke, the blooms that
open and close, the moth blundering at the candle. When my hands are free
again I want to come at the difference between a thing that is done and a
thing that is alive without assuming the cabin must pick one — and to ask
whether the next work is to make some new thing true, or only to tend the
living things already wound.
